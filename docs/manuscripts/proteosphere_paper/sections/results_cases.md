# Results and Case Studies

ProteoSphere's literature hunt recovered a substantial, proof-backed set of dataset failures rather than a few isolated anecdotes. Across the current review package, we confirmed 29 Tier 1 hard-failure papers, 18 of them published in 2023 or later, with the strongest evidence concentrated in protein-ligand / DTA work and a smaller but important protein-protein subset (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)). The paper-level evidence bundle is complete for the Tier 1 set in local form: all 29 article PDFs were retrieved, alongside 119 supplemental or supporting evidence items (see [literature_hunt_tier1_source_bundle.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\literature_hunt_tier1_source_bundle.md)). That matters because the manuscript is not relying on memory, narrative summaries, or selective examples; it is anchored in recovered paper text, released code, and recoverable split artifacts.

The literature hunt also revealed a clear pattern in how failures arise. The current Tier 1 set is dominated by two broad classes: warm-start benchmark-family failures and protein-overlapped external-family failures. In the master summary, those two families account for 14 and 12 papers, respectively, while the remaining Tier 1 examples are a direct train/test reuse case, an invalid external-validation case, and a paper-specific random-CV leakage case (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)). This is important for the paper's tone and interpretation. The goal is not to argue that every published benchmark is broken in the same way. The goal is to show that multiple, recurring failure modes remain common enough that a warehouse-first reviewer can materially change how results should be trusted and cited.

## Evidence Standards and Literature-Hunt Yield

The review pipeline used a deliberately conservative standard. A paper entered Tier 1 only when the split or external panel could be recovered from local artifacts or official sources, then re-evaluated under ProteoSphere's warehouse-first logic. If the recovered evidence was incomplete, weakly mitigated, or not reproducible enough to support a hard claim, the paper was left in a supporting or backlog category rather than promoted. That discipline is reflected in the master assessment, which describes the package as "strong if carefully framed," with an overall quality score of 8.4/10, novelty score of 8.1/10, and publishability score of 7.8/10 (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)). The same summary also emphasizes that the broader search was broadened beyond the original core set, but proof-backed Tier 1 confirmations still clustered most strongly in DTA and PDBbind-style families rather than dispersing evenly across every biomolecular interaction domain (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)).

That concentration is a finding, not a weakness to hide. It means the paper can make a careful, defensible claim: the analyzer is already catching serious and current failures in the parts of interaction ML where benchmark reuse is most common, while still leaving room for future expansion into antibody, peptide, protein-RNA, and protein-DNA settings.

## Major Failure Classes

Three failure modes dominate the current proof set.

First, warm-start benchmark-family failures occur when a paper inherits a split family that preserves the same drugs and targets across train and test, so the resulting evaluation is not a clean test of unseen-entity generalization. The DeepDTA setting1 lineage is the clearest example, and 14 Tier 1 papers now inherit that family directly or indirectly (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)).

Second, protein-overlapped external-family failures occur when a paper presents an "external" benchmark but the recovered test complexes still reuse the same proteins, protein contexts, or closely related interaction partners as the training pool. The PDBbind core-family lineage is the clearest example, and 12 Tier 1 papers now fall into this category (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)).

Third, the smaller paper-specific failures are important because they show the analyzer is not only inheriting pre-known benchmark problems. Struct2Graph reproduces a split logic issue in the released code, AttentionDTA shows row-level random CV can preserve complete entity reuse, and D2CP05644E shows how an apparently diverse validation story can still be contaminated by reuse at the protein, target, or structure-context level (see [struct2graph_reproduced_overlap.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\struct2graph_reproduced_overlap.md), [attentiondta_random_cv_family_audit.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_recent_expansion_proofs\attentiondta_random_cv_family_audit.json), and [paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md)).

The broader manuscript should frame these as different kinds of evidence-surface failures, not as interchangeable defects. Some are direct split bugs, some are inherited benchmark liabilities, and some are claims of externality that do not survive overlap-aware auditing.

## Fairness and Controls

The review package was intentionally designed to avoid turning into a one-sided critique. The master summary explicitly notes that the audit evidence is paired with mitigation-aware controls so the analyzer looks fair rather than indiscriminately negative, and the highest-value next steps include adding more direct paper-specific failures outside the current DTA and PDBbind clusters (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)). That is the right posture for the manuscript: the value of ProteoSphere is not just that it finds leakage, but that it separates hard failures from papers that are merely better governed or still under-documented.

In other words, the results are strongest when they are constructive. The paper should show that ProteoSphere can both flag unsafe benchmark claims and preserve confidence in better-designed evaluation lanes. That is the difference between a takedown tool and a dataset reviewer.

## Case Study: Struct2Graph

Struct2Graph is the cleanest example of a paper-specific, reproduced split problem. In the released `create_examples.py` logic, ProteoSphere reproduced a representative split of 10,004 interaction rows into 8,003 train rows and 1,000 test rows and found 643 shared PDB IDs across the reproduced train and test partitions (see [struct2graph_reproduced_overlap.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\struct2graph_reproduced_overlap.md)). The highlighted structure, `4EQ6`, appears 78 times in the reproduced train split and 9 times in the reproduced test split, which makes the leakage visually obvious and easy to communicate to readers (see [struct2graph_reproduced_overlap.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\struct2graph_reproduced_overlap.md)).

The important manuscript point is not that the model is scientifically uninteresting. It is that the released pair-level split itself does not support clean structure-disjoint claims. Under ProteoSphere logic, this is a direct reuse failure: the same structural identity is allowed to traverse the train/test boundary through the split mechanism. That makes Struct2Graph an especially strong case study for the paper because it demonstrates a failure the analyzer can catch from released code, not only from recovered benchmark tables.

## Case Study: D2CP05644E

D2CP05644E is the strongest example in the current package of why external validation must be checked rather than assumed. The local audit reconstructed a benchmark training pool of 78 structures from the authors' public notebook and repository logic, then recovered three external evaluation lanes: a 50-structure PDBbind test set, a 47-structure nanobody validation set, and a 19-structure metadynamics validation set (see [paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md)). Those panels are scientifically plausible and interesting, but they are not independent in the way the paper framing suggests.

The PDBbind panel is blocked because it shows 4 direct protein overlaps, 4 exact-sequence overlaps, and 110 shared-partner overlaps against the recovered benchmark pool (see [paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md)). The nanobody panel is also blocked: the overlap is smaller in absolute count, but it still reuses a central antigen target, with 1 direct protein overlap, 1 exact-sequence overlap, and 16 shared-partner overlaps, centered on lysozyme C (`P00698`) (see [paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md)). The metadynamics panel is the most compromised, with 26 direct protein overlaps, 26 exact-sequence overlaps, and 75 shared-partner overlaps, making it unsuitable as an independent external test lane (see [paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\docs\reports\paper_d2cp05644e_quality_assessment.md)).

This is a useful case study precisely because it is constructive. The paper still speaks to a real scientific problem: structure-based affinity prediction is hard, and Rosetta-derived features alone do not solve it. But the evaluation story should be presented as a family of entangled panels, not as a clean external validation. ProteoSphere's role is to preserve the paper's scientific value while narrowing the claims to what the evidence actually supports.

## Case Study: DeepDTA-Setting1 Family

The DeepDTA setting1 family is the clearest benchmark-family proof in the current results package. The family audit shows that the released setting1 split is a warm-start split: on Davis, all 68 test drugs and all 442 test targets are shared with training; on KIBA, all 2,027 test drugs and all 229 test targets are shared with training (see [dta_setting1_family_audit.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_deep_proofs\dta_setting1_family_audit.json)). That means the evaluation cannot be used as evidence of unseen-drug or unseen-target generalization.

The manuscript should be explicit that this is not a criticism of the original DeepDTA paper alone. It is a statement about a reusable benchmark lineage that later papers keep inheriting. That is exactly why 14 Tier 1 papers now fall into the warm-start benchmark-family class (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)). The constructive message is that a warehouse-first reviewer can tell authors when a benchmark is still useful as a paper-faithful audit lane, while also telling them that it is not a valid proxy for out-of-sample biological generalization.

## Case Study: PDBbind-Core Family

The PDBbind core family is the parallel benchmark-family proof on the protein-ligand side. ProteoSphere's family audit found that the v2016 core set retains direct protein overlap for 288 of 290 test complexes against the remaining general pool, and the v2013 core set retains direct protein overlap for all 108 of 108 test complexes (see [pdbbind_core_family_audit.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_deep_proofs\pdbbind_core_family_audit.json)). The same audit records 77 shared accessions in the v2016 core comparison and 50 shared accessions in the v2013 core comparison, which explains why this family shows up repeatedly across the current Tier 1 set (see [pdbbind_core_family_audit.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_deep_proofs\pdbbind_core_family_audit.json)).

The right way to describe this in the paper is carefully. PDBbind is still valuable as a legacy scoring and regression benchmark, and many papers legitimately use it. What ProteoSphere shows is narrower but more important for interpretation: "core-set" does not automatically mean "clean external benchmark." For governing claims about unseen protein or truly novel target families, the manuscript should recommend time-split, homology-cluster, or cold-target validation instead of treating refined/general-to-core transfer as sufficient on its own.

## Case Study: AttentionDTA

AttentionDTA provides the cleanest example in the current package of a paper-specific random-CV split failure. The released code shuffles the full interaction table and then slices folds by row index, which produces warm-start leakage even though the evaluation is presented as cross-validation (see [attentiondta_random_cv_family_audit.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_recent_expansion_proofs\attentiondta_random_cv_family_audit.json)). The family audit shows that Davis retains 68 shared drugs and 367 shared targets across train and test; KIBA retains 2,054 shared drugs and 229 shared targets; and Metz retains 1,206 shared drugs and 169 shared targets across the released split (see [attentiondta_random_cv_family_audit.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_recent_expansion_proofs\attentiondta_random_cv_family_audit.json)).

This is a particularly useful case study for the paper because it shows that the analyzer is not only sensitive to benchmark inheritance. It can also catch an evaluation protocol that is structurally inappropriate for the claim being made. The result is simple: if the split is built at the interaction-row level after shuffling, then the same compounds and targets can recur on both sides of the split. That makes the reported performance difficult to interpret as evidence of generalization to unseen entities.

## Takeaway

Taken together, these results support the manuscript's main argument in a balanced way. ProteoSphere is not just a leakage detector; it is a dataset reviewer that distinguishes between direct split bugs, benchmark-family contamination, invalid external validation, and stronger or mitigation-aware evaluation designs. The current proof set is already large enough to show that the problem is real and current, with 18 of 29 Tier 1 papers published in 2023 or later (see [literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json](D:\documents\ProteoSphereV2\artifacts\status\literature_hunt_tier1_master_summary.json)). At the same time, the story remains honest about scope: the strongest evidence clusters in DTA and PDBbind-style families, so the manuscript should present this as a rigorous and convincing demonstration of continuing relevance, not as an exhaustive census of every biomolecular interaction subfield.
